Pillar · Storytelling & Placemaking
1968

I AM A MAN

In 1968, the sanitation workers assembled at Clayborn and marched out carrying signs that declared their humanity. Frontline workers — the first environmental-justice workers — demanding to be seen. This building watched Memphis decide who it was going to be.

"My grandfather assembled here with the sanitation workers in 1968, the morning they handed out the I AM A MAN signs. He kept his sign folded in the family Bible until the day he died."— Marcus Bell, Clayborn Temple · a Memphis Roots memory
The long quiet

Decades of waiting

After the assassination of Dr. King that same year, the light dimmed for the city. Disinvestment settled in. The building that held so much history sat waiting — its roof aging, its stories folded away like a sign in a Bible.

2025 · the stakes

The night it burned

When the community moved to restore Clayborn, the moment of success became a moment of attack. A triple arson tore through the temple and two nearby properties — roughly 75% damage. It could have been the end of the story.

2025 · the turn

The city answered

Instead, Memphis answered. Nearly the entire city amplified the project. Strangers were activated; people came together in tears. The near-loss transformed the work — and drew in stakeholders, governance, and the will to rebuild.

Pillar · Community Ownership & Governance
Today

Rebuilding, on the community's terms

Now the work is restoration — not just of a building, but of a model: shared ownership, equity, a community land trust, a place of refuge and food in a neighborhood the market forgot. And right beside the temple sit 2.5 acres waiting for a purpose.
